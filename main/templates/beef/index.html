The beef with beef

Beef and the climate

  • Greenhouse gases re-emit infrared radiation in the earth's atmosphere - this is contributing to the ongoing global warming effect.
  • The five main greenhouse gas types are carbon dioxide, methane, water vapour, nitrous oxide and fluorinated gases.
  • Although methane has a shorter lifetime than carbon dioxide, it reflects about 100x more heat than carbon dioxide - making it the second most imporant greenhouse gas.
  • 40% of worldwide methane emissions are from agriculture according to the national geographic.
  • Cattle are widely known as some of the largest methane emitters. In this story, I investigate why beef is causing such a problem...

Holding governments accountable...

  • I want to use forecasting to determine what UK agricultural methane emissions may look like in 2030.
  • I selected 2030 as my benchmark due to being only 5 years into the future. This therefore doesn't reduce the reliability of extrapolation as much as more distant predictions.
  • According to the UN website on climate change, greenhouse emissions must peak before 2025 and decline by 43% by 2030.
  • These targets were made in 2016, so I will compare historical methane emissions with forecast methane emissions to determine whether the UK agricultural industry is on-track towards these goals.

The UK beef landscape...

(Click to expand)

  • Dairy cows are reared to produce milk, conversely, beef cows are raised for their meat and therefore farmers prioritise size and musculature.
  • One potential cause for this phenomonon, could be the greater musculature of beef cows requiring more feed.
  • Methane is produced due to cows (like other ruminants) having a four-chambered stomach. Microbial fermentation in the rumen chamber creates volatile fatty acids and methane: the later is released into the cow's environment.
  • Therefore, larger animals which require more food will also produce greater amounts of methane.
  • To caveat this, there is ongoing work to modify the amounts of methane produced by cattle - including dietary modifications, genetic selection and methane capture.

    • Note that this data is only for cows (not the full herd population including calves).

    Whilst net production fluctuates...

    Resulting in the relative value of beef skyrocketing!

  • The value of British cattle has increased dramatically over the last 50 years, however, the mass is roughly constant. For consumers, this creates an issue: beef is worth more than ever before.
  • This rise in the value beef has as simple cause. Supply cannot keep up with demand - therefore a farmers' herd is worth more now than ever.

    • We are actually eating less beef!

  • The British public are responding to the rise in price of beef by actually reducing their consumption!
  • There is a distinct rise in cheese consumption, however, as discussed earlier - the use of dairy cows is far less problematic for methane emissions than beef consumption.

    What does this all mean...

    • Ultimately, I want to forecast UK cattle methane emissions in 2030
    • As the above trends have demonstrated, there are multiple interacting exogenous variables.
    • I therefore created an ARIMA model including the most important of these variables.
    • Predictably, there were significant positive associations between more cows(both dairy and beef) with methane emissions.
    • By creating small future forecasts into 2030 for each of the significant exogenous variables, I could use the data to model cattle emissions in 2030 - as shown below.
  • I used SARIMAX prediction which runs on the Autoregressive Integrated Moving Average with Exogenous Variables.
  • I forecast the future levels of five important predictors (exogenous variables): the population of beef cows, the population of dairy cows, the household consumption of beef and veal /g, the household consumption of wholemilk/ml, and the value per unit mass of cattle
  • I then used the predictors to inform how methane emissions would change in the lead up to 2030.
  • In 2030, total cattle emissions are currently forecast to be at 732.97 kt, seeing a significant reduction from the 1980s. But is this enough?

    • Improvement - but not enough!

    • It seems that people are consuming less beef - perhaps because the price of beef has risen significantly in recent decades.
    • The rearing of cattle for meat purposes is far more environmentally damaging that rearing cattle for dairy.
    • Overall, emissions are reducing in the UK. This is good. However, following current trends, the rate of decrease will level off.
    • A 46% reduction from 2016 would equate to 417kt of methane emissions by 2030.
    • On current trajectories, we will be at 732.92kt in 2030. This is still far too high.
    • However, there is reason for optimism. With ongoing scientific research, we may be able to better manage cattle methane production. Further to this, amidst recent calls for a meat tax, there may be a general movement towards a lower methane UK.
