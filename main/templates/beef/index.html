How the beef with beef is slowly ending

Beef and the climate

  • Greenhouse gases are responsible for re-emitting infrared radiation in the earth's atmosphere - this is contributing to the ongoing global warming effect.
  • The five main greenhouse gases are carbon dioxide, methane, water vapour, nitrous oxide and fluorinated gases.
  • Although methane has a shorter lifetime than carbon dioxide, it reflects about 100x more heat than carbon dioxide - making it the second most imporant greenhouse gas.
  • 40% of methane emissions are from agriculture
  • Cattle are widely known as some of the largest methane emitters. In this story, I investigate why beef is causing such a problem...

Dairy cows produce milk, farmers select for the maximum amount of milk produced. Conversely, beef cows are raised for their meat and therefore farmers prioritise size and musculature.

Click to expand:

One potential cause for this phenomonon, could be the greater musculature of beef cows requiring more feed.

Methane is produced due to cows (like other ruminants) having a four-chambered stomach. Microbial fermentation in the rumen chamber creates volatile fatty acids and methane: the later is released into the cow's environment.

Therefore, larger animals which require more food will also produce greater amounts of methane.

To caveat this, there is ongoing work to modify the amounts of methane produced by cattle - including dietary modifications, genetic selection and methane capture.

Note that this data is only for cows (not the full herd population including calves).

Whilst production mass fluctuates...

The price of beef skyrockets:

The value of British cattle has increased dramatically over the last 50 years, however, the mass is roughly constant. For consumers, this creates an issue: beef is worth more than ever before.

This rise in the value beef has as simple cause. Supply cannot keep up with demand - therefore producer stock from farmers is worth more now than ever.

We are actually eating less beef!

The British public are responding to the rise in price of beef by actually reducing their consumption!

There is a distinct rise in cheese consumption, however, as discussed earlier - the use of dairy cows is far less problematic for methane emissions than beef consumption.

I used SARIMAX prediction which runs on the Autiregressive Integrated Moving Average with Exogenous Variables.

Essentially, I forecast the future levels of five important predictors (exogenous variables): the population of beef cows, the population of dairy cows, the household consumption of beef and veal /g, the household consumption of wholemilk/ml, and the value per unit mass of cattle

I then used the predictors as exogenous variables to inform how methane emissions would change in the lead up to 2030.

2030 is the UK government's target year to achieve its climate goals: including halving global emissions.

Improvement - but not enough!

  • It seems that people are consuming less beef - perhaps because the price of beef has risen significantly in recent decades.
  • The rearing of cattle for meat purposes is far more environmentally damaging that rearing cattle for dairy.
  • Overall, emissions are reducing in the UK. This is good. However, following current trends, the rate of decrease will level off.
  • However, there is reason for optimism. With ongoing scientific research, we may be able to better manage cattle methane production. Further to this, amidst recent calls for a meat tax, there may be a general movement towards a lower methane UK.

How I made this:

I found ARIMA predictions for each variable until 2030... 

              
beef.index = beef.Year
sarimax_model = SARIMAX(beef['Beef Cows'], order=(1, 1, 1))
results = sarimax_model.fit()
forecastBEEF = results.get_forecast(steps=8)
predicted_BEEF = forecastBEEF.predicted_mean
row = list(predicted_BEEF)
print(row)
